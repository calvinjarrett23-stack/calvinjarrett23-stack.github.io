Costco: Leveraged Recapitalization

A $5B debt issuance creates +$1.25B in tax shield value and retires 5.0M shares — but is EPS-dilutive (−1.43% at $5B). Management would likely not pursue it: Costco's leadership treats near-zero leverage as a strategic asset, not an inefficiency.

New debt tranche $5.0B IG notes at 5.5%
WACC — 80/20 illustrative 7.79% → ≈6.7%
EPS impact −1.43% at $5B
Pro forma leverage 0.5x Debt/EBITDA

Problem

Costco runs one of the most conservative balance sheets in large-cap retail: financed almost entirely with equity, rated A+, and holding roughly $15B of cash. This case analysis asks whether the company should pursue a leveraged recapitalization — issuing new debt to fund a return of capital — and what that would do to its cost of capital, per-share economics, and credit profile.

The approach

Built a pro forma recapitalization model: sized a new debt tranche, re-levered the capital structure, and traced the effects through WACC, EPS, and leverage metrics.

  • Debt sizing and structure: $5B of investment-grade notes at 5.5% interest, deployed through a share buyback, with sensitivities modeled up to $25B across five $5B rounds.
  • Cost of capital: WACC falls from 7.79% to roughly 6.7% at an illustrative 80/20 debt/equity target — a bull-case ceiling, not the outcome of the $5B recommendation itself.
  • Per-share impact: the $5B buyback retires 5.0M shares but is EPS-dilutive at −1.43%, offset by a +$1.25B present value of interest tax shield.
  • Credit: times-interest-earned falls from 67x to roughly 55x — still far above distress levels — with pro forma leverage of 0.5x Debt/EBITDA and the A+ rating preserved.

My specific role

Team project, with the analysis split roughly evenly across members.

Outcome

Graded course project; received a high grade. The analysis concluded that the $5B recap creates +$1.25B of tax shield value and retires 5.0M shares while remaining EPS-dilutive at −1.43% — value-accretive on the balance sheet, and still not something management would do.

Why this doesn't actually happen

The model says the recap adds value. The judgment call is why Costco still wouldn't do it: management has no direct incentive to change a capital structure that has worked for decades, and stockholders are wary of upsetting a proven track record — COST returned +169.72% against +70.47% for the S&P 500 over five years. There is a clear information asymmetry between what the math supports and what leadership will actually do. Costco's leadership treats near-zero leverage as a strategic asset, not an inefficiency.
