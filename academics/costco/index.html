Costco: Leveraged Recapitalization

A $5B debt issuance creates +$1.25B in tax shield value and retires 5.0M shares — but is EPS-dilutive (−1.43% at $5B). Management would likely not pursue it: Costco's leadership treats near-zero leverage as a strategic asset, not an inefficiency.

New debt tranche $5.0B IG notes at 5.5%
WACC — 80/20 illustrative 7.79% → ≈6.7%
EPS impact −1.43% at $5B
Pro forma leverage 0.5x Debt/EBITDA

Problem

Costco is one of the most deliberately under-levered large companies in America. It generates more than $270B in annual revenue, renews roughly 93% of its members every year — an annuity-like revenue stream most credit analysts would envy — carries an A+ rating, and still finances itself almost entirely with equity while holding roughly $15B of cash.

From a textbook capital-structure perspective, that is a puzzle. Stable cash flows and low operating risk are precisely the conditions under which debt capacity is cheapest to use, and Costco uses almost none of its own. This case analysis asks whether the company should exploit that capacity through a leveraged recapitalization — issuing new debt to fund a return of capital — and traces what the decision would actually do to its cost of capital, its per-share economics, and its credit standing.

The approach

The engine of the analysis is a pro forma recapitalization model: size a new debt tranche, re-lever the capital structure, and follow the consequences through every metric a board would ask about.

  • Debt sizing and structure. $5B of investment-grade notes priced at 5.5%, deployed entirely into a share repurchase. Rather than resting on a single point estimate, the model stress-tests the structure across five successive $5B rounds — mapping the full path from a conservative first tranche to an aggressive $25B cumulative recap.
  • Cost of capital. Re-weighting toward cheaper after-tax debt pulls WACC from 7.79% toward roughly 6.7%. That floor is measured at an illustrative 80/20 debt-to-equity target — the bull-case ceiling for the thesis, not the outcome of the $5B recommendation itself.
  • Per-share impact. The arithmetic cuts against the popular intuition that buybacks automatically lift EPS. At $5B, retiring 5.0M shares does not outrun the new after-tax interest burden: the recap is EPS-dilutive at −1.43%. The value case rests instead on the balance sheet — roughly +$1.25B in present value of interest tax shields.
  • Credit. Pro forma leverage rises to just 0.5x Debt/EBITDA, and times-interest-earned falls from 67x to roughly 55x — a decline that sounds dramatic and means almost nothing at that altitude. The A+ rating survives comfortably.

My specific role

A graded team project, with the modeling and analysis split roughly evenly across members.

Outcome

Graded course project; received a high grade. More interesting than the grade is the shape of the conclusion: the recap is value-accretive on the balance sheet — +$1.25B of tax shield value against a negligible increase in distress risk — and still fails the EPS test at −1.43%. That tension between balance-sheet value and per-share optics is exactly what makes the capital-structure question a judgment call rather than a mechanical one.

Why this doesn't actually happen

The model says the recap adds value. The judgment call is why Costco still wouldn't do it. Management has no direct incentive to alter a structure that has compounded successfully for decades — COST returned +169.72% over the past five years against +70.47% for the S&P 500, and nothing disciplines financial engineering like a shareholder base that is already being paid for its patience. A membership model built on trust also raises the soft cost of anything that reads as balance-sheet aggression.

The deeper lesson is about the information asymmetry between what the math supports and what leadership will actually do. The spreadsheet can be right and the recommendation still dead on arrival, because Costco's leadership treats near-zero leverage not as an inefficiency to be optimized away but as a strategic asset: optionality, credibility with members and creditors, and insurance against the cycle.
